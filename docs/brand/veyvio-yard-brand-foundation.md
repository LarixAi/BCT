# 2. What Veyvio Yard is

Veyvio Yard is the operational control point for everything that happens to a vehicle before it enters service, while it is in the depot and when it returns.

It helps transport teams answer five important questions:

1. Where is the vehicle?
2. What condition is it in?
3. Is it safe and ready for service?
4. What equipment is assigned to it?
5. What needs attention before it can move?

Veyvio Yard should feel like the digital equivalent of an experienced yard manager who knows every vehicle, notices every problem and keeps the operation moving safely.

# 4. The problem Veyvio Yard solves

Many transport yards still depend on a mixture of paper checklists, WhatsApp messages, spreadsheets, verbal instructions, photographs stored on personal phones and individual staff knowledge.

This creates operational risk.

A vehicle can be moved without a clear record. Damage can be discovered without knowing when it happened. Equipment can disappear without accountability. A defect may be discussed but never formally recorded. A vehicle can appear available even though an important check is incomplete.

Veyvio Yard replaces fragmented information with a clear operational record.

It connects:

* Vehicle identity
* Yard location
* Vehicle condition
* Bodywork evidence
* Daily checks
* Equipment assignments
* Defects
* VOR decisions
* Vehicle movements
* Cleaning and fuelling
* Maintenance handovers
* Driver reports
* Return-to-service approval
* Audit history

The product does not simply record activity.

It creates operational confidence.

# 6. Primary users

## Yard managers

They need to understand the entire yard quickly, assign work, inspect vehicles, manage defects and decide whether vehicles are ready for service.

## Yard operatives

They need simple, guided workflows for checks, vehicle movements, fuelling, cleaning, equipment and defect reporting.

## Transport controllers

They need reliable vehicle availability information so they can protect routes, replace unavailable vehicles and avoid last-minute disruption.

## Maintenance teams

They need clear defect evidence, vehicle history, VOR status and structured handovers.

## Drivers

They need confidence that the vehicle assigned to them has been checked and that any defect they report will reach the correct team.

## Operations managers

They need accountability, audit trails, readiness reporting and visibility across multiple depots.

# 7. Brand idea

## The central idea

**Operational confidence**

# 8. Brand pillars

## Visibility

People should be able to see what is happening without chasing colleagues for updates.

The system should clearly show:

* Where vehicles are
* Which vehicles are available
* Which checks are incomplete
* Which defects are open
* Which vehicles are VOR
* Which vehicles are awaiting attention
* Which actions are overdue

## Readiness

The system should help teams prepare vehicles for service, not merely record that tasks were completed.

Readiness includes:

* Safety
* Cleanliness
* Fuel or charge level
* Required equipment
* Accessibility equipment
* Documentation
* Defect clearance
* Operational approval

## Accountability

Every important action should have an owner, time, location and audit history.

Veyvio Yard should make it clear:

* Who performed the check
* Who moved the vehicle
* Who reported the damage
* Who marked the vehicle VOR
* Who approved its return to service
* What evidence supported the decision

## Confidence

The product should help users feel informed and in control.

A yard manager should never have to guess whether a vehicle is ready.

A controller should not need to call the yard to confirm availability.

A driver should not be left wondering whether a reported defect has been received.

# 9. Brand personality

Veyvio Yard should be:

## Calm

The interface should remain controlled, even when the yard is busy.

It should not overwhelm users with alerts, colours or unnecessary information.

## Decisive

Actions and statuses should be unambiguous.

Use:

* Mark vehicle VOR
* Move vehicle
* Assign equipment
* Record damage
* Approve return to service

Avoid vague actions such as:

* Continue
* Update
* Process
* Manage
* Submit data

## Observant

The system should notice incomplete work, conflicting statuses, overdue actions and missing evidence.

## Dependable

The product must feel trustworthy enough to support real operational and safety decisions.

## Practical

Veyvio Yard is designed for people working around vehicles, outdoors, under time pressure and sometimes with poor connectivity.

## Human

The language should support the user without sounding childish, overly friendly or robotic.

# 11. Tone of voice

## Core voice

**Clear, calm, direct and operational.**

The language should communicate the same way a strong transport supervisor communicates during a busy shift: clearly, respectfully and with no uncertainty about what must happen next.

## Voice principles

### Lead with the operational meaning

Instead of:

“Status successfully updated.”

Use:

“Vehicle marked VOR.”

### Explain what is blocking progress

Instead of:

“Unable to complete.”

Use:

“Rear light check is still incomplete.”

### Use specific vehicle information

Instead of:

“The vehicle has been moved.”

Use:

“LX24 ABC moved to Bay 7.”

### Make responsibility clear

Instead of:

“This needs attention.”

Use:

“Assign this defect to Maintenance.”

### Remain calm during serious events

Instead of:

“CRITICAL ERROR! VEHICLE UNSAFE!”

Use:

“Vehicle cannot enter service. A safety-critical defect is open.”

# 12. Language rules

## Use plain operational language

Preferred terms:

* Vehicle
* Fleet
* Yard
* Depot
* Bay
* Check
* Defect
* Damage
* VOR
* Ready for service
* Return to service
* Equipment
* Assigned
* Removed
* Reported
* Confirmed
* Approved
* Overdue

## Explain specialist terms

The first use of a specialist abbreviation should include the full term.

Example:

**Vehicle off road (VOR)**

After that, the interface can display:

**VOR**

## Avoid technical system language

Do not show users:

* Entity updated
* Record ID
* Mutation failed
* Synchronisation object
* Invalid state transition
* API unavailable
* Database request failed

Translate system behaviour into operational language.

Example:

**Your update has been saved on this device and will sync when the connection returns.**

# 13. Example microcopy

## Successful actions

* Vehicle check completed
* Damage record added
* Equipment assigned
* Vehicle moved to Bay 4
* Vehicle marked VOR
* Return to service approved
* Photos saved
* Defect sent to Maintenance

## Warnings

* This vehicle has an open safety defect
* A required check is still incomplete
* Two equipment items have not been confirmed
* This vehicle is assigned to a route in 45 minutes
* The latest bodywork photographs are more than 90 days old
* You are about to change the vehicle’s service status

## Offline messages

* You are working offline
* Changes are being saved on this device
* Three updates are waiting to sync
* Connection restored. Updates are syncing
* All updates are now synced

## Empty states

* No vehicles require attention
* No open damage records
* No equipment has been assigned
* No yard movements recorded today
* No vehicles are currently VOR

## Confirmation language

Avoid asking for confirmation after every routine action.

Use confirmation for high-impact decisions such as:

* Marking a vehicle VOR
* Returning a vehicle to service
* Removing safety equipment
* Overriding a failed check
* Deleting evidence
* Cancelling an active movement

Example:

**Mark LX24 ABC as VOR?**

This will remove the vehicle from the available fleet and notify Operations.

Recommended layout:

**VEYVIO**
**YARD**

“Veyvio” remains the dominant master brand.

“Yard” acts as the product identifier.

* Do not create a completely separate Yard logo.
* Do not replace the Veyvio symbol with a vehicle icon.
* Do not use crossed tools, steering wheels or generic map pins.
* Do not place “Yard” inside a badge.

The Veyvio Yard app icon should contain:

* The Veyvio route symbol
* A dark navy background
* A Yard teal accent
* No text
* No small vehicle illustration
* Strong recognition at small sizes

The icon should still be recognisable when displayed beside other Veyvio applications.

# 21. Motion and interaction

Motion should communicate state changes, not decorate the interface.

Recommended motion:

* A short confirmation when a check is completed
* A vehicle card moving between yard zones
* A progress ring updating after a completed section
* A status chip transitioning from “Checking” to “Ready”
* A subtle sync indicator when offline updates upload
* A controlled warning transition when a safety issue is found

Avoid:

* Bouncing buttons
* Long page transitions
* Celebration animations
* Excessive confetti
* Distracting loading effects
* Motion that slows down repeat operational work

---

# 22. Haptic feedback

On supported devices, haptic feedback can reinforce important actions.

Use a light confirmation for:

* Barcode or QR scan completed
* Photograph captured
* Check item confirmed
* Equipment assignment completed

Use stronger feedback for:

* Safety-critical defect recorded
* Vehicle marked VOR
* Return-to-service decision
* Failed mandatory check

Haptics should never replace visible confirmation.

# 23. Product experience principles

## Status before statistics

The user should first see what requires attention.

Do not make the home screen primarily about charts.

The first questions are:

* What is unsafe?
* What is late?
* What is unavailable?
* What needs to move?
* What is required before service?

## Exceptions before routine work

Vehicles requiring action should appear above vehicles with no issues.

## Evidence before assumption

Damage, defects and return-to-service decisions should include supporting evidence where appropriate.

## One clear primary action

Each screen should have one obvious next action.

## Large, practical controls

The app may be used:

* Outdoors
* In gloves
* While walking
* In poor weather
* Beside moving vehicles
* In low-light conditions

Controls must be easy to select without precision tapping.

## Make offline status visible

Users must always know whether their work has synced.

## Never hide safety-critical information

A critical defect must remain visible until properly resolved.

## Separate observation from approval

The person who records that work has been completed may not always be the person authorised to approve return to service.
